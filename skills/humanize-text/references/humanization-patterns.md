## AI Writing Patterns to Avoid

### Overused AI Vocabulary

These words and phrases appear disproportionately in AI-generated text:

**Tier 1 - Extremely Common AI Markers**:
- "delve" / "delve into" → Use: explore, examine, look into, dive into, investigate
- "leverage" → Use: use, utilize, take advantage of, employ
- "robust" → Use: strong, solid, reliable, powerful, comprehensive
- "paradigm" → Use: model, approach, framework, method
- "synergy" → Use: cooperation, teamwork, collaboration
- "utilize" → Use: use (almost always)
- "facilitate" → Use: help, enable, make easier, support

**Tier 2 - Frequent AI Phrases**:
- "it's important to note that" → Usually can be removed entirely
- "it should be noted that" → Remove or use "note that"
- "it's worth mentioning" → Remove or rephrase directly
- "in order to" → Use: to
- "due to the fact that" → Use: because, since
- "at this point in time" → Use: now, currently
- "in the event that" → Use: if
- "prior to" → Use: before
- "subsequent to" → Use: after
- "with regard to" → Use: about, regarding
- "in terms of" → Often removable or use "for" / "in"

**Tier 3 - Overly Formal Constructions**:
- "aforementioned" → Use: this, that, the
- "the aforementioned solution" → Use: this solution
- "exemplify" → Use: show, demonstrate, illustrate
- "ascertain" → Use: find out, determine, learn
- "endeavor" → Use: try, attempt, effort
- "terminate" → Use: end, stop, finish
- "commence" → Use: start, begin
- "purchase" → Use: buy (in casual contexts)

### Structural AI Patterns

**1. Excessive Use of Passive Voice**:
- ❌ "The solution was implemented by the team"
- ✅ "The team implemented the solution"

**2. Overly Complex Sentence Structures**:
- ❌ "The implementation of the system, which was designed to enhance operational efficiency, resulted in significant improvements."
- ✅ "The new system enhanced operations significantly."

**3. Redundant Intensifiers**:
- ❌ "very unique", "extremely essential", "completely eliminate"
- ✅ "unique", "essential", "eliminate"

**4. Formulaic Openings**:
- ❌ "In today's fast-paced world..."
- ❌ "It is widely acknowledged that..."
- ❌ "As we navigate the complexities of..."
- ✅ Start directly with the point

**5. Mechanical Transitions**:
- Using "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally," in sequence
- Solution: Mix in casual transitions like "Also," "Plus," "And," "What's more"

### Repetitive Patterns

**Paragraph-Level Repetition**:
- Starting multiple paragraphs with the same structure
- Using identical transition words repeatedly
- Maintaining uniform paragraph length throughout

**Sentence-Level Repetition**:
- Every sentence following subject-verb-object pattern
- Similar sentence lengths throughout
- Predictable punctuation patterns

## Human Writing Patterns to Emulate

### Natural Sentence Variety

**Mix Short and Long**:
```
Short punchy sentence. Followed by a longer, more descriptive sentence that provides context and detail. Then another short one. And maybe a medium-length sentence to balance things out.
```

**Vary Opening Structures**:
- Subject-first: "The solution works well."
- Prepositional phrase: "In most cases, it works well."
- Adverb: "Surprisingly, it works well."
- Conjunction: "But it works well."
- Participle: "Working well, the solution exceeded expectations."

### Natural Contractions

Use contractions to match formality level:

**Casual/Conversational** (use liberally):
- don't, can't, won't, shouldn't, you're, it's, we'll, they're

**Professional** (use selectively):
- don't, can't, won't (common in business writing)
- Avoid: gonna, wanna, gotta

**Formal** (use sparingly or avoid):
- Minimal contractions, full forms preferred

### Strategic Imperfections

Humans naturally include:

**1. Mild Hedging** (when appropriate):
- "This might work better"
- "It seems like a good approach"
- "Probably the best option"

**2. Conversational Asides**:
- "Now, this is interesting..."
- "Here's the thing..."
- "To be fair..."
- "That said..."

**3. Rhetorical Questions**:
- "Why does this matter?"
- "What's the alternative?"
- "Sound familiar?"

**4. Direct Address**:
- "You might be wondering..."
- "Let me explain..."
- "Consider this..."

### Personality Injection

**Casual Tone Markers**:
- "pretty good" instead of "quite adequate"
- "a lot of" instead of "numerous"
- "really" instead of "very"
- "kind of" / "sort of" (used sparingly)

**Conversational Tone Markers**:
- Questions directed at reader
- Second-person "you" 
- First-person "I" or "we"
- Informal examples and analogies

**Professional-but-Warm Markers**:
- "We recommend" instead of "It is recommended"
- "This helps you" instead of "This provides the user with"
- "Let's look at" instead of "An examination of"

## Context-Specific Guidance

### Email Writing

**Business Email - Professional Tone**:
- Use contractions sparingly (don't, can't are fine; shouldn't use informal ones)
- Keep paragraphs short (2-4 sentences)
- Direct, action-oriented language
- Warm but professional sign-offs

**Casual Email**:
- More contractions allowed
- Conversational language
- Can use fragments for emphasis
- Friendly, personalized tone

### Blog Posts

**Engaging Blog Style**:
- Mix paragraph lengths (1-5 sentences)
- Use subheadings liberally
- Ask rhetorical questions
- Include personal anecdotes or examples
- Conversational tone with occasional humor
- Strong, clear opening hook

**Technical Blog**:
- Explain complex concepts simply
- Use analogies and examples
- "Technical-but-friendly" tone
- Code examples with plain-language explanations
- Conversational without being too casual

### Social Media

**Twitter/X**:
- Extremely concise
- Punchy, often fragment sentences
- Strong hooks
- Casual language
- Emoji appropriate for brand voice

**LinkedIn**:
- Professional but personable
- Storytelling elements
- Industry-relevant insights
- Call-to-action
- More formal than Twitter, less formal than white paper

### Documentation

**User-Facing Docs**:
- Clear, simple language
- Active voice
- Step-by-step instructions
- "You will" instead of "The user will"
- Helpful, supportive tone

**Technical Docs**:
- Precise terminology
- Consistent formatting
- Less conversational
- Focus on accuracy over personality

## Voice Consistency Tips

### Establishing Voice

**Define Key Characteristics**:
1. **Formality Level**: Casual ↔ Professional ↔ Formal
2. **Personality**: Friendly, authoritative, playful, serious, warm
3. **Expertise**: Beginner-friendly, expert-to-expert, educational
4. **Energy**: Enthusiastic, measured, passionate, calm

### Maintaining Consistency

**Vocabulary Consistency**:
- Choose level of technical jargon and stick with it
- Don't switch between "purchase" and "buy" randomly
- Maintain consistent contractions usage

**Structural Consistency**:
- Keep similar types of content in similar formats
- Maintain heading hierarchy
- Use consistent list formatting

**Tone Consistency**:
- Don't shift from casual to formal mid-piece
- Keep humor level consistent
- Maintain consistent level of directness

### Red Flags for Inconsistency

- Switching between "you" and "one"
- Mixing contractions and formal constructions randomly
- Varying technical depth without reason
- Inconsistent use of industry jargon

## Common AI Overused Words and Phrases

### Words to Watch

**Overused Adjectives**:
- seamless → smooth, easy, integrated
- innovative → new, creative, fresh
- cutting-edge → latest, advanced, modern
- revolutionary → groundbreaking, transformative (use sparingly)
- game-changing → significant, impactful
- robust → strong, reliable, solid
- comprehensive → complete, thorough, full

**Overused Verbs**:
- streamline → simplify, improve, optimize
- optimize → improve, enhance, refine
- enhance → improve, boost, strengthen
- facilitate → enable, help, support
- implement → use, apply, put in place
- leverage → use, utilize, employ
- utilize → use (almost always just "use")

**Overused Adverbs**:
- significantly → greatly, substantially, a lot
- effectively → well, successfully
- efficiently → quickly, smoothly
- seamlessly → smoothly, easily

### Phrases to Replace

**Wordy Phrases**:
- "in the process of" → currently, now
- "has the ability to" → can
- "is able to" → can
- "in a timely manner" → promptly, quickly, on time
- "with the exception of" → except
- "for the reason that" → because
- "on a regular basis" → regularly
- "in spite of the fact that" → although, despite

## Natural Alternatives Database

### Transition Words by Tone

**Casual Alternatives**:
- Furthermore → Also, Plus, And
- Moreover → What's more, On top of that
- Therefore → So, That's why
- However → But, Yet, Though
- Subsequently → Then, Next, After that
- Additionally → Also, Plus, And

**Professional Alternatives**:
- Furthermore → Additionally, In addition
- Moreover → Furthermore, Also
- Therefore → Thus, As a result
- However → Nevertheless, Nonetheless
- Subsequently → Following this, Next
- Additionally → In addition, Further

**Conversational Alternatives**:
- Furthermore → And here's another thing
- Moreover → Plus
- Therefore → So here's the thing
- However → But here's the catch
- Subsequently → Then
- Additionally → Oh, and

## Sentence Structure Patterns

### Varied Sentence Types

**1. Simple Sentences** (one independent clause):
- "The system works well."
- Use for: Emphasis, clarity, pacing

**2. Compound Sentences** (two independent clauses):
- "The system works well, and users love it."
- Use for: Connecting related ideas

**3. Complex Sentences** (independent + dependent clause):
- "Because the system works well, users love it."
- Use for: Showing relationships, cause/effect

**4. Compound-Complex** (multiple independent + dependent):
- "Because the system works well, users love it, and adoption rates are high."
- Use for: Detailed explanations (use sparingly)

### Rhythm and Flow

**Create Rhythm Through Length Variation**:
```
Short. Medium length sentence. A longer sentence that provides more detail and context. Short again.
```

**Use Punctuation for Pacing**:
- Periods: Full stop, emphasis
- Commas: Brief pause, lists
- Semicolons: Longer pause, related ideas
- Dashes: Interruption, emphasis — like this
- Parentheses: Asides (less emphasis)

## Transition Word Variety

### Building Connection

**Addition**:
- AI-typical: Furthermore, Moreover, Additionally
- Human variety: Also, Plus, And, What's more, On top of that, Beyond that

**Contrast**:
- AI-typical: However, Nevertheless, Nonetheless
- Human variety: But, Yet, Though, Still, Even so, That said

**Cause/Effect**:
- AI-typical: Therefore, Consequently, Thus
- Human variety: So, That's why, As a result, Because of this

**Examples**:
- AI-typical: For instance, For example
- Human variety: Like, Such as, Take [X], Consider

**Time**:
- AI-typical: Subsequently, Prior to, Following
- Human variety: Then, Next, After, Before, Later, Soon

**Emphasis**:
- AI-typical: Indeed, Certainly, Undoubtedly
- Human variety: In fact, Actually, Really, Clearly

### Strategic Use

- **Don't overuse any single transition word**
- **Mix formal and casual based on overall tone**
- **Sometimes no transition is needed** - ideas can flow naturally
- **Vary placement** - not every transition needs to start the sentence
